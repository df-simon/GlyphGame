Script Traditions

Why these pronunciations?

Many writing systems are thousands of years old, and their letters have changed pronunciation as languages evolved.

Scryble focuses on older and more conservative pronunciation traditions that preserve historical sound distinctions whenever possible.

The goal is not to show the most common modern pronunciation.

The goal is to reveal the original relationships between scripts and the sounds their letters were designed to represent.

This approach highlights connections between related alphabets:

How Phoenician letters developed into Hebrew, Arabic, Greek, Latin, Cyrillic, and other writing systems.

Where no single ancient pronunciation survives, Scryble uses a traditional or historically informed pronunciation that best represents the character of the script.

Arabic

Scryble uses Classical Arabic pronunciation, the traditional pronunciation used for Qur'anic Arabic and formal study.

Examples:
ج Jim : /d͡ʒ/ "j" sound. Many modern dialects: /g/ in Egyptian Arabic or /ʒ/ in parts of the Levant.

ق Qaf : /q/ deep Q sound. Many modern dialects: /ʔ/ glottal stop, /g/, or /k/.

ث Tha : /θ/ "th" sound. Many modern dialects merge it with /t/ or /s/.

Hebrew

Scryble uses Yemenite Hebrew and the reconstructed Tiberian tradition, which preserve older Semitic sounds.

Examples:
ו Waw : /w/. Modern Israeli Hebrew (Vav): usually /v/.

ח Het : /ħ/ deep throat H. Modern Israeli Hebrew: usually /χ/ like German Bach.

ע Ayin : /ʕ/ throat consonant. Modern Israeli Hebrew: usually silent.

ק Qof : /q/. Modern Israeli Hebrew: usually merged with /k/.

Greek

Scryble uses historical Greek pronunciations to show older sound values preserved closer to Ancient Greek.

Examples:
Β Beta : /b/. Modern Greek: /v/.

Δ Delta : /d/. Modern Greek: /ð/ like "th" in this.

Γ Gamma : /g/. Modern Greek: often /ɣ/.

Η Eta : /e/. Modern Greek: /i/.

Latin

Scryble uses Classical Latin pronunciation from the Roman period, instead of modern church or English-influenced pronunciation.

Examples:
V : /w/ as in "wine". Later Latin traditions: /v/.

C : always /k/. Later pronunciations: sometimes /s/ or /t͡ʃ/.

G : always hard /g/. Modern languages often soften it before certain vowels.

AE : "eye" sound. Later traditions: often "eh".

Cyrillic

Scryble uses a historically informed Slavic pronunciation, based on traditional Cyrillic sound values and older Slavic distinctions.

Modern Cyrillic languages have developed in different directions, and some letters have shifted, merged, or disappeared. Scryble preserves the older relationship between the letters and the sounds they originally represented.

Examples:
Г Ge : /g/ hard G sound.
Ukrainian: shifted to /ɦ/.

Ѣ Yat (historical Cyrillic) → Older Slavic: separate vowel.
Modern Russian: merged with Е.
Other Slavic languages developed different reflexes.

Щ Shcha : preserves a distinct historical Slavic sound.
Modern languages differ: Russian /ɕː/, Bulgarian /ʃt/.

Ge'ez

Scryble uses Classical Ge'ez pronunciation, the ancient liturgical language of Ethiopia and Eritrea. This preserves older Ethiosemitic and Afroasiatic sound distinctions.

Many modern Ethiosemitic languages descended from or related to Ge'ez have simplified some of these older consonant distinctions. Scryble keeps the more conservative traditional forms.

Examples:
ሠ Śä : preserves a separate historical ś sound.
Modern Amharic: merged with ሰ Sä.

ኀ Ḫä : preserves the older guttural sound.
Modern Amharic: mostly merged with ሀ Hä.

ዐ ʿÄyn : preserves the Semitic throat consonant /ʕ/.
Modern Amharic: generally lost as a distinct consonant.

ጠ Ṭä / ቀ Qä / ጸ Ṣä : preserves ejective consonants.
Modern Ethiosemitic languages still preserve many ejectives, but individual sounds have shifted differently.

Phoenician

Based on reconstructed Classical Phoenician pronunciation from the first millennium BCE. Phoenician was a Northwest Semitic abjad and one of the most influential writing systems in history.

Scryble uses reconstructed older Semitic pronunciations, preserving sounds that show the relationship between Phoenician and later scripts. Some descendants preserved these sounds, while others adapted the letters for very different languages.

Examples:

𐤀 Aleph represented the glottal stop /ʔ/. This sound was preserved in Semitic descendants such as Hebrew Aleph (א) and Arabic Hamza (ء), while Greek transformed Aleph into the vowel Alpha (Α), ancestor of Latin A.

𐤅 Waw represented /w/. This pronunciation survives in Classical Arabic Waw (و) and older Hebrew traditions, while some later Hebrew traditions shifted it toward /v/. Through Greek and Latin development, it also contributed to letters such as F, U, V, W, and Y.

𐤇 Het represented the deep throat sound /ħ/. This survives in Arabic Ḥa (ح) and traditional Semitic pronunciations, while Greek Eta (Η) lost the original consonant value.

𐤈 Tet represented an emphatic /tˤ/. This sound is preserved in Arabic Ṭa (ط) and related Semitic traditions, while Greek adapted the letter into Theta (Θ).

𐤏 Ayin represented the voiced throat sound /ʕ/. This survives in Arabic Ayn (ع) and older Hebrew traditions, while Greek adapted the letter into Omicron (Ο), changing it into a vowel.

𐤒 Qoph represented the uvular /q/. This survives in Arabic Qaf (ق) and some traditional Hebrew pronunciations. It also became Greek Qoppa (Ϙ) and eventually Latin Q.
