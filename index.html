Script Traditions

Why these pronunciations?

Many writing systems are thousands of years old, and their characters have
changed pronunciation as languages evolved.

Scryble focuses on older and more conservative pronunciation traditions
that preserve historical sound distinctions whenever possible.
The goal is not always to show the most common modern pronunciation,
but to reveal the relationships between scripts and the sounds their
characters were originally designed to represent.

Where no single ancient pronunciation survives, Scryble uses a traditional
or historically informed pronunciation that best represents the character
of the script.



Arabic

Scryble uses Classical Arabic pronunciation,
the pronunciation associated with Qur'anic recitation, early Arabic literature, and formal study.
Arabic is a Central Semitic language written with an abjad descended from the Nabataean Aramaic script,
which ultimately traces back through Aramaic to the Phoenician alphabet.

The Arabic script developed in the Arabian Peninsula,
especially in what is today Saudi Arabia and Jordan, from earlier Nabataean writing used in the northern Arabian region.
By the rise of Islam in the 7th century CE, Arabic writing spread rapidly beyond Arabia.

At its peak, Arabic became one of the most widespread writing systems in the world,
used from the Iberian Peninsula (Spain and Portugal) and North Africa, across the Middle East and Persia (Iran),
into Central Asia, South Asia, and parts of Southeast Asia and Africa.
Today the Arabic script is used for Arabic and many other languages
including Persian, Urdu, Pashto, and others.

Examples:

ا Alif : /aː/
Long "aa" vowel carrier.
Descended from Phoenician 𐤀 Aleph through Aramaic and Nabataean.
Related historically to Hebrew א and Greek Alpha Α.

ب Ba : /b/
B sound.
Descended from Phoenician 𐤁 Bet through Aramaic and Nabataean.
Related historically to Hebrew ב, Greek Beta Β, and Latin B.

ج Jim : /d͡ʒ/
"J" sound as in traditional Classical Arabic pronunciation.
Many modern dialects: /g/ in Egyptian Arabic or /ʒ/ in parts of the Levant and North Africa.

ح Ha : /ħ/
Voiceless pharyngeal sound.
Preserves an ancient Semitic sound also found in Phoenician 𐤇 Het and older Hebrew ח pronunciation.

خ Kha : /x/
Rough "kh" sound.
Developed from an ancient Semitic sound distinction.
In Arabic writing, dots were later added to distinguish characters such as ح and خ.

ط Ta : /tˤ/
Emphatic T sound.
Preserves an ancient Semitic sound related to Phoenician 𐤈 Tet and Hebrew ט.

ع Ayn : /ʕ/
Voiced pharyngeal sound.
Preserves an ancient Semitic sound found in Phoenician 𐤏 Ayin and older Hebrew ע pronunciation.

ق Qaf : /q/
Deep uvular Q sound.
Preserves an ancient Semitic sound related to Phoenician 𐤒 Qoph and Hebrew ק.
Many modern dialects pronounce it as /ʔ/, /g/, or sometimes /k/.

ث Tha : /θ/
"Th" sound as in English "thin."
Preserves an ancient Semitic sound. Many modern dialects merge it with /t/ or /s/.

ذ Dhal : /ð/
"Th" sound as in English "this."
Many modern dialects merge it with /d/ or /z/.

ش Shin : /ʃ/
"Sh" sound.
Related to Phoenician 𐤔 Shin and Hebrew ש.
The same ancient character family also produced Greek Sigma Σ and Latin S.

Hebrew

Scryble uses pronunciation based on Yemenite Hebrew and reconstructed Tiberian Hebrew,
which preserve or reconstruct many older Semitic sound distinctions that were lost in most modern varieties.

Hebrew is a Northwest Semitic language closely related to Phoenician.
The Hebrew alphabet developed from the ancient Phoenician script through the Paleo-Hebrew alphabet,
later adopting the Aramaic square script during and after the Babylonian exile.
This developed into the Hebrew script used today.

Hebrew originated in the southern Levant among the ancient Israelites,
especially in the regions of modern Israel and Palestine,
with related ancient Hebrew-speaking communities in surrounding areas such as Jordan.

During antiquity, Hebrew writing was used mainly in the southern Levant.
Through Jewish communities after the diaspora, the Hebrew script spread across the
Middle East, North Africa, Europe, Central Asia, and later the Americas,
where it was used for Hebrew and Jewish languages such as Yiddish, Ladino, Judeo-Arabic, and others.

After declining as a daily spoken language in antiquity,
Hebrew survived for centuries as a religious, literary, and scholarly language
before being revived as a modern spoken language in the 19th–20th centuries.

Examples:

א Aleph : /ʔ/
Glottal stop.
Preserves an ancient Semitic sound related to Phoenician 𐤀 Aleph and Arabic ء.
Modern Israeli Hebrew often weakens or leaves it silent.

ב Bet : /b/
B sound.
Descended from Phoenician 𐤁 Bet.
Related historically to Arabic ب, Greek Beta Β, and Latin B.

ו Waw : /w/
W sound in older Hebrew traditions including Yemenite Hebrew.
Related to Phoenician 𐤅 Waw and Arabic و.
Modern Israeli Hebrew (Vav) usually pronounces it /v/.

ח Het : /ħ/
Voiceless pharyngeal sound.
Preserved in Yemenite Hebrew and related to Phoenician 𐤇 Het and Arabic ح.
Modern Israeli Hebrew usually merges it with /χ/, similar to Khaf כ.

ט Tet : /tˤ/
Emphatic T sound in older Semitic pronunciation.
Related to Phoenician 𐤈 Tet and Arabic ط.
Most modern Hebrew traditions pronounce it as plain /t/.

ע Ayin : /ʕ/
Voiced pharyngeal sound.
Preserved in Yemenite and some Mizrahi traditions.
Related to Phoenician 𐤏 Ayin and Arabic ع.
Modern Israeli Hebrew usually weakens it or leaves it silent.

ק Qof : /q/
Deep uvular Q sound in older Semitic pronunciation.
Related to Phoenician 𐤒 Qoph and Arabic ق.
Modern Israeli Hebrew usually merges it with /k/.

ש Shin : /ʃ/
"Sh" sound.
Preserves the ancient Semitic sound found in Phoenician 𐤔 Shin and Arabic ش.
Related historically to Greek Sigma Σ and Latin S.

Greek

Scryble uses reconstructed Classical Greek pronunciation,
especially the Attic pronunciation used in Athens during the 5th–4th centuries BCE,
to show older Greek sound values before later changes.

The Greek alphabet developed around the 8th century BCE from the Phoenician alphabet.
Greeks adapted the Northwest Semitic abjad by adding dedicated vowel letters,
creating one of the earliest true alphabets with separate symbols for both consonants and vowels.

The Greek alphabet originated among Greek-speaking communities around the Aegean Sea,
especially in what is today Greece, western Turkey, and the Greek islands.

Through Greek colonization and later the conquests of Alexander the Great,
the Greek alphabet and language spread throughout the eastern Mediterranean and Near East.
At its peak, Greek writing was used from southern Europe and Egypt across the Middle East
into Persia (Iran), Central Asia, and parts of South Asia.

Greek became one of the most influential writing systems in history, giving rise to or influencing many later alphabets
including the Latin, Cyrillic, Coptic, Gothic, and Armenian scripts.

Examples:

Α Alpha : /a/
A sound.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤀 Aleph, originally a consonant /ʔ/.
Greek transformed the unused consonant into a vowel letter. Later produced Latin A.

Β Beta : /b/
B sound in Classical Greek.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤁 Bet. Later produced Latin B.
Modern Greek pronounces Beta as /v/.

Γ Gamma : /g/
G sound in Classical Greek.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤂 Gimel. Later produced Latin C and G.
Modern Greek usually pronounces Gamma as /ɣ/ or /ʝ/.

Δ Delta : /d/
D sound in Classical Greek.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤃 Dalet. Later produced Latin D.
Modern Greek pronounces Delta as /ð/.

Η Eta : /ɛː/
Long "eh" vowel in Classical Greek.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤇 Het, originally a consonant.
Some early Greek dialects used it for /h/ before it became a vowel.
Modern Greek pronounces Eta as /i/.

Θ Theta : /tʰ/
Aspirated T sound in Classical Greek.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤈 Tet.
Later changed into the /θ/ "th" sound found in Modern Greek.

Λ Lambda : /l/
L sound.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤋 Lamed. Later produced Latin L.

Ο Omicron : /o/
Short O sound.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤏 Ayin, originally a pharyngeal consonant /ʕ/.
Greek transformed it into a vowel because Greek lacked the original sound.

Ρ Rho : /r/
Rolled R sound.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤓 Resh. Later produced Latin R.

Σ Sigma : /s/
S sound.
Adapted from Phoenician 𐤔 Shin. Later produced Latin S.

Latin

Scryble uses reconstructed Classical Latin pronunciation
from the late Roman Republic and early Roman Empire, preserving older Roman sound values before later changes in Medieval Latin and the Romance languages.

The Latin alphabet developed from the Old Italic scripts of ancient Italy,
especially the Etruscan alphabet, which was itself adapted from the Greek alphabet.
Through Greek, the Latin alphabet ultimately traces back to the Phoenician script.

Latin originated in the region of Latium in central Italy, especially around the city of Rome.
Early Latin was originally spoken by a small Italic community before expanding with the growth of the Roman Republic and Roman Empire.

At its peak, Latin writing spread across the entire Roman world, from Britain and western Europe through North Africa, the Balkans, Egypt, and the Middle East.
After the fall of the Western Roman Empire, Latin continued as the language of scholarship, religion, science, and law across much of Europe.

The Latin alphabet became the most widely used writing system in the world.
It gave rise to the scripts used for the Romance languages and was later adapted for many others,
including English, German, Vietnamese, Turkish, Indonesian, Swahili, and hundreds of modern languages.

Examples:

A : /a/
Open A sound.
Derived from Greek Alpha Α, ultimately from Phoenician 𐤀 Aleph.
Later became the letter A used throughout Latin-based alphabets.

B : /b/
B sound.
Derived from Greek Beta Β, ultimately from Phoenician 𐤁 Bet.

C : /k/
Always a hard K sound in Classical Latin.
Derived from Greek Gamma Γ through Etruscan.
Later Latin and Romance languages developed soft pronunciations such as /s/ and /t͡ʃ/.

G : /g/
Always a hard G sound in Classical Latin.
Created by modifying C to distinguish /g/ from /k/.
Many modern languages later developed softened pronunciations.

V : /w/
W sound as in English "wine" in Classical Latin.
Derived from Greek Upsilon Υ.
Later Latin traditions changed this sound into /v/.
The single Roman letter V eventually developed into the separate letters U, V, and W.

I : /i/ and /j/
Used for both the vowel "ee" sound and the consonant Y sound as in "yes."
Later developed into separate letters I and J.

AE : /ae̯/
Diphthong similar to "ai" in Classical Latin.
Later Latin traditions merged it into an "eh" sound /ɛː/.

OE : /oe̯/
Diphthong sound in Classical Latin.
Later merged with /e/ in many pronunciations.

R : /r/
Rolled R sound.
Derived from Greek Rho Ρ, ultimately from Phoenician 𐤓 Resh.

S : /s/
S sound.
Derived from Greek Sigma Σ, ultimately from Phoenician 𐤔 Shin.

Cyrillic

Scryble uses a historically informed pronunciation based on Old Church Slavonic and Early Cyrillic traditions,
preserving older Slavic sound values before many later changes in modern Slavic languages.

The Cyrillic alphabet was developed in the late 9th–early 10th century CE in the First Bulgarian Empire
by students of the Byzantine missionaries Cyril and Methodius.
It was based mainly on the Greek alphabet, with additional letters created for Slavic sounds not found in Greek.

The Cyrillic script originated in the Balkans, especially in what is today Bulgaria and North Macedonia,
among Slavic-speaking Christian communities.

Through religion, literature, and political expansion, Cyrillic spread throughout Eastern Europe and northern Asia.
At its peak it was used across the Balkans, Russia, Ukraine, Belarus, Siberia, Central Asia, the Caucasus, and parts of East Asia.

Cyrillic became one of the world's major writing systems and is used today for many languages including
Russian, Ukrainian, Bulgarian, Serbian, Macedonian, Mongolian, Kazakh, Kyrgyz, and others.

Examples:

А Az : /a/
A sound.
Adapted from Greek Alpha Α, ultimately from Phoenician 𐤀 Aleph through the Greek alphabet.

В Vedi : /v/
V sound.
Adapted from Greek Beta Β, which had shifted from ancient /b/ to /v/ by the Byzantine period.

Г Glagoli : /g/
Hard G sound in Old Church Slavonic and many Cyrillic languages.
Adapted from Greek Gamma Γ.
Ukrainian later shifted this sound to /ɦ/.

Д Dobro : /d/
D sound.
Adapted from Greek Delta Δ, ultimately related to Phoenician 𐤃 Dalet.

Ж Zhivete : /ʒ/
"Zh" sound as in "vision."
Created for a Slavic sound not represented in Greek.

И Izhe : /i/
"Ee" vowel sound.
Adapted from Greek Eta Η, which had shifted to /i/ by the Byzantine period.

Ѣ Yat : /ě/
Historical Slavic vowel letter representing a distinct sound in Old Church Slavonic.
Later merged differently across Slavic languages: with Е in Russian, І/Ї-related sounds in Ukrainian, and Я/Е sounds in Bulgarian dialects.

Ц Tsi : /t͡s/
"Ts" sound.
Added to represent a Slavic sound not found in Classical Greek.

Ч Cherv : /t͡ʃ/
"Ch" sound.
Added for a Slavic sound not represented by Greek letters.

Ш Sha : /ʃ/
"Sh" sound.
Created for Slavic pronunciation needs.

Щ Shcha : /ʃt/ or /ʃtʃ/
Originally represented a distinct Slavic consonant cluster.
Modern languages developed it differently: Russian /ɕː/, Bulgarian /ʃt/.

Ъ Yer : /ŭ/
Originally represented a short reduced vowel in Old Church Slavonic.
In modern Russian it became the hard sign with no independent vowel sound.

Ь Yer : /ĭ/
Originally represented a short reduced vowel in Old Church Slavonic.
In modern Russian it became the soft sign marking palatalization.

Geʿez

Scryble uses historically informed Classical Geʿez pronunciation,
based on the traditional liturgical pronunciation of the Ethiopian and Eritrean churches and reconstruction of older Ethiosemitic sound distinctions.

Geʿez is a South Semitic language written with the Geʿez script (Fidel).
The script developed from the ancient South Arabian writing tradition, which ultimately traces back to the early Semitic alphabet family.
Unlike most Semitic scripts, Geʿez developed from an abjad into an abugida,
where each character represents a consonant combined with a vowel.

Geʿez originated in the Horn of Africa, especially the ancient Kingdom of Aksum, located in what is today northern Ethiopia and Eritrea.
It became the language of administration, inscriptions, literature, and religion in the Aksumite civilization.

At its peak, Geʿez writing was used across the Horn of Africa, especially Ethiopia, Eritrea, and regions connected through Red Sea trade with Arabia.
Although it declined as a daily spoken language, it survived as a sacred and literary language.

The Geʿez script remains one of the world's oldest continuously used writing systems and is still used today for languages including
Amharic, Tigrinya, Tigre, and other Ethiopian and Eritrean languages.

Examples:

ሀ Hä : /h/
H sound.
Preserves a Semitic consonant related to Arabic ه and Hebrew ה.

ሠ Śä : /ɬ/ or /sʼ/
Historical ś sound inherited from older Semitic.
In modern Amharic, it merged with ሰ Sä.

ሰ Sä : /s/
S sound.
Related to other Semitic sibilants such as Arabic س and Hebrew ס.

ኀ Ḫä : /ħ/
Older guttural H sound.
Related to Arabic ح and Hebrew ח.
In modern Amharic, it mostly merged with ሀ Hä.

ዐ ʿÄyn : /ʕ/
Voiced pharyngeal sound.
Related to Arabic ع, Hebrew ע, and Phoenician 𐤏 Ayin.
Modern Amharic generally lost this pronunciation.

ቀ Qä : /kʼ/
Ejective Q sound.
Preserves the older Semitic emphatic consonant series.
Related historically to Arabic ق and Hebrew ק.

ጠ Ṭä : /tʼ/
Ejective T sound.
Related to the emphatic consonants of other Semitic languages such as Arabic ط and Hebrew ט.

ጸ Ṣä : /sʼ/
Ejective S sound.
Preserves an ancient Semitic emphatic consonant distinction.

ፐ Pä : /p/
P sound.
A later addition to represent sounds needed in Ethiopian languages.
Classical Semitic languages often lacked /p/.

Phoenician

Based on reconstructed first-millennium BCE Phoenician pronunciation.
Phoenician was a Northwest Semitic abjad developed from earlier Proto-Canaanite writing.
It originated on the eastern Mediterranean coast of the Levant,
especially in the Phoenician city-states of Byblos, Sidon, and Tyre in what is today mainly Lebanon,
with related Phoenician-speaking areas extending into parts of modern Syria and northern Israel.

Through Phoenician trade and colonization (c. 1000–300 BCE),
the script spread across the Mediterranean world.
At its peak it was used from the Levant to Cyprus, Turkey, Greece, Malta, Sicily, Sardinia,
Southern France, North Africa (especially Tunisia and Carthage), and the Iberian Peninsula (modern Spain and Portugal).

Phoenician became one of the most influential writing systems in history, giving rise to or influencing many later writing systems
including the Hebrew, Aramaic, Greek, Latin, and Arabic alphabets.

Examples:

𐤀 Aleph : /ʔ/.
Glottal stop.
Preserved in Hebrew א and Arabic ء.
Greek adapted the consonant letter into the vowel Alpha Α.

𐤁 Bet : /b/
B sound.
Preserved in Hebrew ב and Arabic ب.
Greek adapted it into Beta Β, which later produced Latin B.

𐤅 Waw : /w/.
W sound.
Preserved in Classical Arabic و and older Hebrew pronunciation.
Later developments produced Greek Digamma Ϝ and Latin F,
while another Greek adaptation through Upsilon Υ eventually led to U, V, and W.

𐤇 Het : /ħ/.
Voiceless pharyngeal sound.
Preserved in Arabic ح and older Hebrew traditions.
Greek Eta Η preserved it briefly as an /h/ sound in some dialects before later becoming a vowel.

𐤈 Tet : /tˤ/.
Emphatic T sound.
Preserved in Arabic ط.
Greek adapted it into Theta Θ, originally pronounced /tʰ/.

𐤏 Ayin : /ʕ/.
Voiced pharyngeal sound.
Preserved in Arabic ع and older Hebrew traditions.
Greek adapted the unfamiliar consonant sign into the vowel Omicron Ο.

𐤒 Qoph : /q/.
Deep uvular Q sound.
Preserved in Arabic ق and some Semitic traditions.
Adapted into Greek Qoppa Ϙ and later Latin Q.

𐤔 Shin : /ʃ/.
"Sh" sound.
Preserved in Hebrew ש and Arabic ش.
Greek adapted the Semitic sibilant into Sigma Σ, which later produced Latin S.
